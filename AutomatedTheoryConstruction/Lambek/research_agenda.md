# Research Agenda for the Lambek Calculus: Toward the Construction of a Structural Theory

This document sets out research guidelines for obtaining structural results that form the core of the theory of the Lambek calculus ($L$) and its extensions, rather than merely accumulating isolated computational examples.

---

## 1. Themes

* **Structural correspondences between language hierarchies and logical systems**
  Discover logical systems that correspond to meaningful classes of formal languages within the hierarchies generated by adding, removing, or modifying structural rules in substructural logics.
* **Extraction of logical rules and complexity**
  Identify logical rules that characterize specific language classes, and give rigorous characterizations of the computational complexity that accompanies them.

---

## 2. Valued Problem Types

Within this agenda, solutions to problems of the following kinds are especially valuable.

* **Provability relations among structural rules (reducibility)**
  Prove whether a rule $A$ can realize a transformation equivalent to rule $B$ by combining other rules.
  > **Example:** rather than introducing commutativity in a fully symmetric form, introduce asymmetric exchange rules whose effect depends on operator directionality; for example, allowing $A/B, B, C$ to be transformed into $A/B, C, B$, while forbidding a transformation to $B, A/B, C$.
* **Discovery of invertibility**
  Identify rules or systems such that, whenever the conclusion can be derived from the premises, the premises can in turn be derived unchanged from the conclusion.
* **Separability**
  For one logical system (a set $A$ of structural rules) and another logical system (a set $B$ of structural rules), find propositions that are provable in $A$ but not in $B$, thereby giving a rigorous separation of the expressive power of $A$ and $B$.
* **Proofs of metatheorems**
  Determine whether a given new extension of a logical system admits a proof of the cut-elimination theorem.
* **Exact correspondences with language classes**
  Discover proof systems in which the acceptability of a language class is equivalent to provability in a particular logic associated with that language.

---

## 3. Anti-Goals

The following kinds of results do not materially advance the theory and should be avoided.

* **Cosmetic rewrites**
  Research that merely restates existing proofs in different notation, or proposals for extended systems that do not involve any substantive structural change and amount only to renaming symbols.
* **Unprincipled proliferation of structural rules**
  Introducing new structural rules on an ad hoc basis solely to fit particular computational outcomes, without solid algebraic justification or logical symmetry. This leads to uncontrolled growth in the complexity of the system and makes fundamental metatheorems such as cut elimination and decidability harder to establish.
* **One-off examples**
  Adding special rules or parameters introduced only to explain a narrow language phenomenon, such as a rare word-order pattern in a particular language, with no genuine applicability in other contexts.
* **Unproductive negative statements**
  Producing large numbers of negative, instance-specific claims of the form "a certain theorem is not derivable." Such results do not, by themselves, contribute directly to the construction of systematic correspondences.

---

## 4. Canonical Targets

These are the most important objectives: results whose resolution would force a fundamental rewriting of the standard summary of the theory.

1. **A deeper understanding of completeness for $L$**
   Clarify the limits of completeness theorems with respect to specific classes of models, such as finite models or language models, and identify the algebraic and structural reasons behind those limits.
2. **The boundary between cut elimination and decidability in extensions**
   Identify the sharp boundary at which decidability is lost when structural rules such as exchange, weakening, contraction, or restricted versions of them are introduced only partially.
3. **The interface with categorial grammar**
   Formulate structural preservation properties for mappings from syntactic types to semantic representations, viewed as an extension of the Curry-Howard correspondence.

---

## 5. Soft Constraints

* **Closeness to established notation and theory**
  Whenever possible, retain standard Lambek notation such as $A \backslash B$, $B / A$, and $A \bullet B$ in order to preserve readability within the community.
* **Temporal durability**
  Prefer claims of sufficiently high abstraction that will remain valuable even as new theorems and methods are introduced. Avoid excessive dependence on fashionable computational techniques, and instead ground results in the essential structure of the logic itself.
