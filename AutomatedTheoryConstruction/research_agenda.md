# Research Agenda (Default)

## Themes

- Prefer problems that reorganize the current theory rather than merely extending its local envelope.
- Prefer problems that clarify what the current theory is really about.
- Prefer results that expose structure, boundaries, or governing principles over isolated calculations.

## Valued Problem Types

- Criterion theorems that decide when the main phenomenon occurs.
- Converse / non-converse pairs that separate genuinely different regimes.
- Bridge results connecting notions, hypotheses, or proof patterns that are currently disconnected.
- Classification, rigidity, or uniqueness statements that compress many local facts into one structural picture.
- Sharp boundary statements showing necessity, sufficiency, or exact failure modes.

## Anti-Goals

- Variants of existing lemmas that only tweak hypotheses, notation, or proof packaging.
- Shallow corollaries whose main value is local closure or bookkeeping.
- One-off examples whose role is only to verify a nearby fact.
- Problems that remain inside already overexplored proof patterns without changing the theory summary.

## Canonical Targets

- A result that would force the top-level summary of the theory to be rewritten.
- A result that explains the current phenomenon through a single organizing principle.
- A result that turns scattered examples and counterexamples into a general criterion.
- A result that reveals a sharp boundary, not just another sufficient condition.
- A result that connects two currently separate parts of the theory in a reusable way.

## Soft Constraints

- Stay close enough to the active theory that proposed statements remain testable in the current development.
- Permit bolder jumps when they directly address a bottleneck or missing bridge in the theory.
- When choosing between a safe peripheral extension and a summary-changing statement, prefer the latter.
- Prefer statements likely to remain meaningful after several more theorems are added.
