# Research Agenda for the Lambek Calculus: Toward the Construction of a Structural Theory

This document sets out research guidelines for obtaining structural results that reorganize the theory of the Lambek calculus and its extensions, rather than merely accumulating isolated computational examples.

## 1. Themes

- Clarify the structural taxonomy of Lambek systems by identifying which extensions represent genuinely distinct proof-theoretic regimes and which amount to reducible variants.
- Determine exact correspondences among structural rules, semantic model classes, and formal-language classes, so that proof theory, algebra, and generative capacity constrain one another sharply.
- Identify the metatheoretic boundaries at which cut elimination, decidability, completeness, and complexity change, especially under partial or directional control of structural principles.
- Formulate the syntax-semantics interface of categorial grammar in structural terms, so that semantic interpretation preserves proof-theoretic distinctions rather than collapsing them.

## 2. Valued Problem Types

- Clarify derivability, admissibility, reducibility, and independence relations among structural rules and rule schemata for $L$ and its principled extensions.
- Determine when structural or logical rules are invertible, admissible, or normalization-preserving, and isolate the exact role these properties play in metatheoretic control.
- Identify separation theorems that rigorously distinguish extensions by formulas, derivability classes, model classes, or language classes, rather than by isolated examples.
- Formulate exact boundary results for cut elimination, decidability, and complexity under restricted exchange, weakening, contraction, and directional or partial variants thereof.
- Discover completeness and incompleteness theorems relative to semantically natural model classes, together with structural explanations for why particular completeness claims succeed or fail.
- Determine exact correspondences between logically natural Lambek systems and robust classes of formal languages, with proofs that connect expressive power to identifiable structural principles.
- Clarify which translations from syntactic derivations to semantic representations preserve structural invariants, and which erase distinctions that the theory should retain.

## 3. Anti-Goals

- Avoid cosmetic reformulations that merely rename connectives, restate existing proofs in different notation, or package known systems without introducing new structural understanding.
- Avoid ad hoc proliferation of structural rules introduced only to fit isolated computational or linguistic effects, without algebraic necessity, symmetry, or metatheoretic justification.
- Avoid one-off examples, special parameters, and local variants whose only purpose is to capture a narrow phenomenon and which do not contribute to a reusable classification.
- Avoid collections of negative instance results that do not culminate in a separation theorem, obstruction principle, or exact boundary statement.
- Avoid fashionable technical machinery when it does not sharpen the structural theory, improve exactness, or force a reorganization of the standard account.

## 4. Canonical Targets

1. Determine the structural classification of Lambek extensions generated by controlled additions of exchange, weakening, contraction, and related directional principles.
   A resolution should yield a durable map of which extensions are equivalent, reducible, incomparable, or genuinely new.
2. Identify the exact boundary at which cut elimination, decidability, and complexity fail or change across disciplined families of Lambek extensions.
   A resolution should replace scattered case analyses by a structural threshold theorem.
3. Formulate a sharp completeness and incompleteness theory for $L$ and its major extensions over semantically natural model classes.
   A resolution should explain not only whether completeness holds, but which structural features force its success or obstruction.
4. Discover a structural account of the interface between Lambek derivations and categorial semantics that preserves the essential asymmetries of the calculus.
   A resolution should recast the standard summary of the syntax-semantics relation in proof-theoretic rather than merely representational terms.

## 5. Soft Constraints

- Prefer standard Lambek notation and conservative extensions of it, so that new results remain legible as contributions to the established theory rather than as notation-driven rebrandings.
- Prefer exact classifications, equivalence theorems, separation results, and boundary theorems over accumulations of examples, constructions, or case-specific derivations.
- Prefer abstractions that remain valuable under future changes of method, and avoid dependence on transient computational fashions or tool-specific implementations.
- Prefer structural principles that are algebraically motivated, proof-theoretically disciplined, and semantically interpretable, rather than tuned to produce isolated outcomes.
