初めに

About six years ago, when I was a high school student, I often felt overwhelmed by the number of advanced mathematics books lined up on the shelves of large bookstores. At the same time, I was fascinated by how such complex theories could be derived from relatively small sets of axioms.

Since then, I have been interested in the idea of generating rich mathematical structures automatically from simple foundations. However, classical automated theorem proving has been limited in its ability to explore and construct such theories at a high level.

Recently, the mathematical capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have improved significantly. They can assist with proofs and sometimes suggest new perspectives on existing statements. Motivated by this, I started the automated-theory-construction-lean4 repository, aiming to explore how LLMs can complement traditional methods by supporting exploration and conjecture generation in theory construction.

概要

The program is quite simple.

1. First, provide a base axiom and place elementary propositions, which serve as seeds for theory development, into the open problem queue.

2. Retrieve a proposition from the open problem queue and have Codex CLI attempt to formalize either a proof or a counterexample within a fixed time limit.

3. If the proposition is formalized, add it to `Derived.lean` as a theorem.

In addition, use the Codex CLI logs produced during the proof attempt as follows:

- If the proposition is not formalized, put it back into the open problem queue together with its subgoals.
- If it is formalized, generate more general candidate problems and add them to the open problem queue.

Repeat steps 2 and 3.

The prompt for generating more general problems is given below. This is the most important part of the program.

```
When the current problem is solved and verified (`verify_success = true` and `result = proof|counterexample`):
- Prefer outward-looking follow-up problems that extend the theory rather than merely staying near the last proof script.
- Favor, in roughly this order:
  1. natural generalizations or reusable abstractions
  2. converses, strict separations, or failure-of-converse statements
  3. existence, uniqueness, impossibility, or rigidity phenomena
  4. finite-model behavior, extremal behavior, boundary cases, or classification fragments
  5. adjacent structural consequences that clarify the global shape of the theory
- It is good to return at least one candidate that meaningfully broadens, reinterprets, or reuses the verified result beyond the immediate local target.
- Prefer candidates whose resolution would teach something non-obvious about the theory or its models, rather than merely restating the solved fact in slightly altered form.
- If a more informative model-level, structural, or boundary-case follow-up is available, prefer it over a nearby local rewrite.
```

## Detailed Mechanism

### Mental Model

The system can be understood as a loop connecting a fixed theory with a verification and expansion pipeline:

- `Theory.lean` defines the base symbols and axioms.
- `run_loop.py` orchestrates generation, formalization, verification, and expansion.
- `Scratch.lean` is used for temporary Lean verification.
- `Derived.lean` accumulates verified theorems over time.

In each iteration, the system attempts to extend the current theory by solving and expanding open problems.

- --

### Runtime Design

The main loop is implemented in `scripts/run_loop.py`. The current implementation uses fixed paths:

- theory: `AutomatedTheoryConstruction/Theory.lean`
- accumulated theorems: `AutomatedTheoryConstruction/Derived.lean`
- temporary verification: `AutomatedTheoryConstruction/Scratch.lean`
- initial seeds: `AutomatedTheoryConstruction/seeds.jsonl`
- runtime state: `data/`

At this stage, the system is not yet a fully generic multi-theory runner. To switch theories, these files must be modified directly.

- --

### Iteration Pipeline

Each iteration proceeds as follows:

1. Select the next open problem deterministically.

2. If the problem is not yet in Lean form, use `prover_statement` to translate it into a formal statement.

3. Use `prover` to attempt a proof, counterexample, or determine that the problem is currently stuck.

4. Run `formalize`, then verify the result using:

`lake env lean AutomatedTheoryConstruction/Scratch.lean`

5. If verification fails, invoke `repair` repeatedly until the retry budget is exhausted.

6. If verification succeeds, append the resulting theorem to `Derived.lean`.

7. Run `expand` to generate additional candidate problems.

8. Update the system state deterministically (`open`, `solved`, `counterexamples`).

Open problems may be either Lean-formal statements or semi-formal natural language prompts. Problems that cannot yet be formalized remain in the queue.

- --

### Three-Stage Formalization

Proof formalization is decomposed into three stages:

1. Formalization of the statement

2. Natural language proof generation

3. Formalization of the proof in Lean

This decomposition helps separate semantic understanding from syntactic verification.

- --

### Problem Generation

New problems can be generated in either natural language or Lean.

Each stage in the pipeline (statement generation, proof generation, formalization, repair, and expansion) is controlled by its own prompt file, allowing independent tuning of behavior.

- --

Overall, the system is designed as an iterative interaction between:

- symbolic verification (Lean), and
- heuristic exploration (LLMs),

with the goal of gradually constructing a coherent theory from simple initial seeds.

今後の目標

The goal is to enable the system to incorporate more general propositions—those that are less tied to a specific internal language—into the theory as theorems.

Ideally, I would like the resulting theories to have a consistent style and structure, similar to `Basic.lean` in mathlib.

In many cases, when I come up with new axiom systems in logic, language theory, or type theory, it is not immediately clear how important they are. As a result, they tend to be deprioritized. This project can be used as a way to explore and understand what kind of theory emerges from such axiom systems.

I plan to continue improving this system in line with advances in AI.

If you have small axiom systems you would like to experiment with, feel free to let me know.
